Coffee Session
Flow Difference
In the latest coffee session, we came back to our best-performing podcast ever with Byron Allen. He is a senior consultant at Servian.

Yea, I Know. Kubeflow and MLflow again. 2022 edition. Conversations around these two tools never end.

What makes MLFlow and KubeFlow? These two MLOps Lifecycle popstars have been topping the trend charts for a couple of years now. Pointing out their differences is key to understanding what made them popular.

At its core, it goes something like this. MLflow is a straightforward package that enables users to handle the experiment phase in the MLOps Lifecycle. KubeFlow is more of an end-to-end platform for MLOps. It's core feature is centered around the orchestration of ML pipelines. Kubeflow pipelines. An abstraction on top of Argo workflows, which we discussed at length a week ago. However, KF is not a one trick pony. It supports much more than pipelines.

Nuances: Kubeflow has a tightly coupled structure that is dependent on Kubernetes and DevOps practices. This makes setup and integration with other tools a bit more rigid. It is ideal when working in a cross-functional team because in practice it's meant to be used by a data scientist/ML engineer, but they might not necessarily be experienced in Dev/Ops.

MLFlow has more of a decoupled structure that enables a very high level of flexibility. It makes it easy to swap out and integrate functionalities with other tools. This gives more liberty to the way it is used.

We break down all the differences and how the tools have evolved in the latest coffee session.

Community Resource
SQL Generator
Rasgo recently launched a free SQL generator.

For those unfamiliar with what a SQL Generator is. Think template for SQL queries, which lets you customize column names and table structure, choose the operation that you want to do, and then, it constructs the syntax for you in different "flavors" of SQL.

Never stress about the subtle differences between DATEDIFF() vs DATE_DIFF() again!

The Story?  People were searching on Google and Stack Overflow for required SQL syntax and wasting precious time that could be used for data analysis. The Rasgo team wanted to help - no strings, paywalls, or email collections attached.

Select the transform you’re running and the column names of your table. The SQL generator will automatically give you the SQL you need in copy/paste form.
